    Worksheet Section 3.4 - Determinants
    

    Objectives

    • How do we think about the determinant of a 2x2 matrix geometrically?
      🔗
      🔗
    • How can we easily compute the determinant of a diagonal (or upper or lower triangular) matrix?
      🔗
      🔗
    • What is \(\det(AB)\) in terms of \(\det(A)\) and \(\det(B)\text{?}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Given \(\det(A)\text{,}\) how can you tell if \(A\) is invertible?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    \\
    🔗
    Recall that the determinant a \(2\times 2\) matrix \(A = [\vvec_1 \vvec_2]\) is the area of the parallelogram formed by \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\text{,}\) with a negative sign if the vectors are negatively oriented. We denote the determinant of \(A\) as \(\det A\text{.}\)
    🔗

    1.

    For this first problem, go to Section 3.4: Determinants, in your textbook \\http://gvsu.edu/s/1rw and scroll down to the applet in Activity 3.4.2 (it will have a blue square and 4 sliders).
    🔗
    The 4 sliders are corresponding to the matrix \(\begin{bmatrix} a \amp b \\ c \amp d \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) If the figure turns red, it means the vectors are negatively oriented.
    🔗

    (a)

    Use the diagram to find the determinant of \(I_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp0 \\ 0 \amp1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Use the diagram to find the determinant of the matrix
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} -\frac{1}{2} \amp0\\ \s 0 \amp2 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    What is the geometric effect of the matrix transformation defined by this matrix? What does this lead you to believe is generally true about the determinant of a diagonal matrix?\footnote{Diagonal means the only non-zero entries are on the diagonal.}
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Use the diagram to find the determinant of the matrix
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp1\\ 1 \amp0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    What is the geometric effect of the matrix transformation defined by this matrix?
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    Use the diagram to find the determinant of the matrix
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp1\\ 0 \amp1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    What is the geometric effect of the matrix transformation defined by this matrix?
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    What do you notice about the determinant of any matrix of the form
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \ampb\\ 0 \amp1 \end{bmatrix}? \end{equation*}
    This matrix is special because it is ``upper triangular", which means all the entries below the diagonal (in this case there is only one), are \(0\text{.}\) What do you think you can say about the determinant of an upper triangular matrix in general?
    🔗
    🔗

    (f)

    Use the diagram to find the determinant of the matrix
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp1\\ 2 \amp1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    What can you say in general about the determinant of matrix that has linearly dependent columns?
    🔗
    🔗

    (g)

    Consider the matrices
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp\s 1\\ 2 \amp-1 \end{bmatrix} , \ \ \ \ B =\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp0\\ 0 \amp2 \end{bmatrix}, \ \ \ \ \text{note \ } AB=\begin{bmatrix}2\amp2\\2\amp-2\end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    Use the diagram to find the determinants of \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) and \(AB\text{.}\) What does this suggest is generally true about the relationship of \(\det(AB)\) to \(\det A\) and \(\det B\text{?}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Here’s some facts we’ll use about determinants which you may have discovered in question 1:
    🔗
    • \(\det I = 1\) (the determinant of the identity is \(1\))
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(A\) is diagonal then \(\det A\) is the product of its diagonal entries. (Since each diagonal entry represents stretching along one of the axes.)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(A\) is upper triangular (or lower triangular), then \(\det A\) is also the product of the entries on the diagonal. (The parallelograms have equal area.)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle \det AB = \det A \det B\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If the columns of \(A\) are linearly dependent then \(\det A = 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    2.

    When we generalize to \(3\) dimensions we end up finding the volume of a parallelpiped. We won’t worry about the geometric details here, but instead we’ll use the fact that the determinant of an upper (or lower) triangular matrix is the product of the entries on the diagonal. Find the determinant of the upper triangular matrix:
    \begin{equation*} U = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 3 \amp 1 \amp -3\\ 0 \amp -2 \amp 2 \\ 0\amp 0 \amp 5 \end{array} \right]. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    Sage can easily find the determinant of a square matrix A with either A.det() or A.determinant(). Find the determinant of the matrix
    \begin{equation*} B = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} -1 \amp 4 \amp 0\\ 6 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ 3 \amp 2 \amp 0 \end{array} \right]. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    We are interested in understanding a connection between the determinant of a matrix and the invertibility of that matrix. To understand this connection, we will study the effect of the three row operations (interchange, scaling, and row replacement) on determinants.
    🔗

    (a)

    Interchange: Take the matrix \(B\) from question 3 and interchange any two rows to obtain a matrix \(B_1\text{.}\) Compute \(\det(B_1)\text{.}\) How does it compare to \(\det(B)\text{?}\) This is what generally happens.
    🔗
    If a matrix \(A\) has a nonzero determinant and we interchange two rows, explain why the determinant of the new matrix is nonzero.
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Scaling: Now scale the first row of \(B\) by \(3\) to obtain the matrix \(B_2\text{.}\) Compute \(\det(B_2)\) and compare it to \(\det(B)\text{.}\) This is also what happens generally.
    🔗
    If a matrix \(A\) has a nonzero determinant and we scale a row by a nonzero number, explain why the determinant of the new matrix is nonzero.
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Row replacement: Finally, perform a row replacement operation\footnote{Add a scalar multiple of one row to another row and replace that row, like we do when row reducing.} on \(B\) to obtain \(B_3\text{.}\) Compute \(\det(B_3)\) and comapre it to \(\det(B)\text{.}\) This is also what generally happens.
    🔗
    If a matrix \(A\) has a nonzero determinant and we perform a row replacement operation, explain why the determinant of the new matrix is nonzero.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    5.

    Using question 4, we’ll determine the relationship between invertibility and determinants:
    🔗

    (a)

    If you have a matrix \(A\) whose determinant is nonzero, what can you guarantee about the determinant of its row echelon form? Explain your thinking.
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Consider the following two \(3\times3\) matrices, both of which are in reduced row echelon form:
    \begin{equation*} R_1 = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 \amp 0 \amp -3 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 4 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ \end{array} \right],\hspace*{24pt} R_2 = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ \end{array} \right]. \end{equation*}
    Find \(\det(R_1)\) and \(\det(R_2)\) (you shouldn’t need Sage to do this).
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    If \(A\) is a \(3\times3\) matrix with a nonzero determinant, which of the two matrices \(R_1\) and \(R_2\) is possible as the reduced row echelon form of \(A\text{?}\) Explain your thinking.
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    If \(A\) is a matrix with nonzero determinant, what can you guarantee about the reduced row echelon form of \(A\text{?}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    If \(A\) a square matrix with a nonzero determinant, explain why \(A\) is invertible.
