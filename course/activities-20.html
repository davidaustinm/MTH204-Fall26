    Worksheet Section 3.2 - Bases and Coordinate Systems
    

    Objectives

    • What is a basis?
      🔗
      🔗
    • How do we determine if a given set of vectors is a basis?
      🔗
      🔗
    • How many vectors are in any basis for \(\real^n\text{?}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • How do we convert from a vector in a non-standard basis \(\mathcal{B}\) to its standard basis coordinates algebraically? (That is, \(\{\xvec\}_{\mathcal{B}}\) to \(\xvec\text{.}\))
      🔗
      🔗
    • How do we convert from a vector in a standard basis to its coordinates in another basis algebraically? (That is, \(\xvec\) to \(\{\xvec\}_{\mathcal{B}}\text{.}\))
      🔗
      🔗
    • How do we think about non-standard bases geometrically?
      🔗
      🔗

    \\
    🔗
    A set of vectors in \(\real^n\) that spans \(\real^n\) and is linearly independent is called a basis of \(\real^n\text{.}\)
    🔗

    1.

    This question is to get you familiar with facts about bases.
    🔗

    (a)

    Explain why the vectors \(\vvec_1=\twovec21\) and \(\vvec_2=\twovec12\) form a basis for \(\real^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Explain why the vectors \(\evec_1\text{,}\) \(\evec_2\text{,}\) and \(\evec_3\) form a basis for \(\real^3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    How many vectors will be in a basis for \(\real^{12}\text{?}\) Explain your thinking. In general, how many vectors will be in a basis for \(\real^n\text{?}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    Suppose that \(\vvec_1,\vvec_2,\ldots,\vvec_n\) is a basis for \(\real^n\text{.}\) Explain why every vector \(\bvec\) in \(\real^n\) can be written as a linear combination of \(\vvec_1,\vvec_2,\ldots,\vvec_n\) in exactly one way.
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    If \(\vvec_1,\vvec_2,\ldots,\vvec_n\) form a basis for \(\real^n\) what is the row reduced echelon form of \(A = [\vvec_1\,\, \vvec_2\, \, \ldots\, \, \vvec_n]\text{?}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (f)

    What can you say about the invertibility of the matrix \(A\) if its columns form a basis?
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2.

    Suppose that \(\vvec_1=\twovec21\) and \(\vvec_2=\twovec12\text{.}\) In part 1a you explained why these vectors form a basis of \(\real^2\text{,}\) and we’ll denote this basis as \(\bcal=\{\vvec_1,\vvec_2\}\text{.}\) Every vector in \(\real^2\) can be expressed in two different ways: in its usual form as a column vector AND as a linear combination of \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\text{.}\) We are going to use this to define a new coordinate system for \(\real^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{center} \includegraphics{basis-1.eps} \end{center}
    🔗
    We can express the vector \(\twovec30\) as linear combination of \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\text{,}\) that is \(\twovec30 = 2\vvec_1 - \vvec_2\text{.}\) (And we can see this using the figure above or using algebra.) In our new coordinate system defined by \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\text{,}\) this vector has coordinates \(2\) and \(-1\text{,}\) that is the first coordinate in our new system is the weight of \(\vvec_1\text{,}\) and the second coordinate is the weight of \(\vvec_2\) when our vector is written as a linear combination of \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\text{.}\) We will write this as
    \begin{equation*} \coords{\twovec{3}0}{\bcal} = \twovec{2}{-1}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    In general, if \(\xvec=c_1\vvec_1+c_2\vvec_2\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \coords{\xvec}{\bcal} = \twovec{c_1}{c_2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    (a)

    Express \(\twovec{-4}1\) as a linear combination of \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\) to find the coordinates \(\coords{\twovec{-4}1}{\bcal}\text{.}\) Do using algebra. Does your answer seem reasonable based on the figure?
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Find the vector \(\xvec\) so that \(\coords{\xvec}{\bcal} = \twovec2{-3}\text{.}\) Can you use the figure first? Then do using algebra? (Notice this is ``opposite" from question (a).)
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Suppose that \(A=\left[\vvec_1 \ \vvec_2 \right]\text{.}\) What is \(A\twovec{-2}3\text{?}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    Consider the vectors
    \begin{equation*} \vvec_1=\threevec10{-2},\hspace*{24pt} \vvec_2=\threevec{-2}1{0},\hspace*{24pt} \vvec_3=\threevec112 \end{equation*}
    🔗

    (a)

    Explain why the vectors form a basis \(\bcal\) for \(\real^3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Find the vector \(\xvec\) such that \(\coords{\xvec}{\bcal} = \threevec2{-1}{-2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Find the coordinates \(\coords{\threevec{-2}{1}{-8}}{\bcal}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    Explain how to convert \(\{\xvec\}_\mathcal{B}\text{,}\) the representation of a vector \(\xvec\) in the coordinates defined by \(\mathcal{B}\text{,}\) into \(\xvec\text{,}\) its representation in the standard coordinate system.\footnote{The representation in the standard coordinate system is with the standard basis vectors \(e_1,\dots, e_n\text{,}\)}
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    Explain how to convert the vector \(\xvec\) into \(\{\xvec\}_\mathcal{B}\text{,}\) its representation in the coordinate system defined by \(\mathcal{B}\text{.}\)
