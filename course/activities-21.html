    Worksheet Section 3.2 - Bases and Coordinate Systems - Part 2
    

    Objectives

    • How do we convert from a vector in a non-standard basis \(\mathcal{B}\) to its standard basis coordinates algebraically? (That is, \(\{\xvec\}_{\mathcal{B}}\) to \(\xvec\text{.}\))
      🔗
      🔗
    • How do we convert from a vector in a standard basis to its coordinates in another basis algebraically? (That is, \(\xvec\) to \(\{\xvec\}_{\mathcal{B}}\text{.}\))
      🔗
      🔗
    • How do we think about non-standard bases geometrically?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    \\
    🔗
    Recall: if \(\bcal = \{\vvec_1, \vvec_2, \ldots, \vvec_n\}\) is a basis for \(\real^n\text{,}\) then if
    \begin{equation*} \xvec = c_1\vvec_1+c_2\vvec_2 + \cdots + c_n\vvec_n, \end{equation*}
    then
    \begin{equation*} \coords{\xvec}{\bcal} = \left[\begin{array}{c}{c_1}\\{c_2}\\ \vdots \\ c_n\end{array}\right]. \end{equation*}
    That is, \(\xvec\) is the normal vector in standard form, and \(\{\xvec\}_{\bcal}\) is the vector of coefficients (weights) of the linear combination of the vectors in the basis \(\bcal\) that equals \(\xvec\text{.}\)
    🔗

    1.

    Suppose that \(\bcal\) is the basis for \(\real^2\) consisting of the vectors \(\vvec_1=\twovec21\) and \(\vvec_2=\twovec12\text{.}\) Let’s form the matrix
    \begin{equation*} P_{\bcal}=\left[\begin{array}{cc}\vvec_1 \amp \vvec_2 \\ \end{array}\right] =\mattwo2112. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    (a)

    Explain why \(P_{\bcal}\coords{\xvec}{\bcal} = \xvec\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Find a matrix \(B\) such that \(B\xvec = \coords{\xvec}{\bcal}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2.

    Computers represent colors using a color model. The simplest is the \(RGB\) color model, which represents colors as a 3-dimensional vector \(\threevec RGB\) describing how much red \(R\text{,}\) green \(G\text{,}\) and blue \(B\) to mix together to create a color. These quantities are represented internally with 8 bytes so, in practice, the range of values is \(0\) to \(255\) for each color, \(R\text{,}\) \(G\text{,}\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    🔗
    A second color model is \(YC_bC_r\text{,}\) which is known as the luminance-chrominance color model. We introduce the basis \(\bcal\) consisting of vectors
    \begin{equation*} \vvec_1 = \threevec 111, \hspace*{24pt} \vvec_2 = \threevec 0{-0.34413}{1.77200}, \hspace*{24pt} \vvec_3 = \threevec {1.40200}{-0.71414}0, \end{equation*}
    and define
    \begin{equation*} \coords{\threevec RGB}{\bcal} = \threevec Y{C_b}{C_r} \end{equation*}
    The quantity \(Y\) is called the luminance and measures the brightness of the color, \(C_b\) is the blue chrominance and measures how much blue is mixed in, and \(C_r\) is the red chrominance and measures how much red is mixed in. The ranges of values are
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} 0 \amp \leq Y \leq 255 \\ -127.5 \amp \leq C_b \leq 127.5 \\ -127.5 \amp \leq C_r \leq 127.5 \\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Go to {http://gvsu.edu/s/0Jc} where you will find some figures to experiment with color models. Remember that \(R\text{,}\) \(G\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) and \(Y\) run between 0 and 255 while \(C_b\) and \(C_r\) run between -127.5 and 127.5.
    🔗

    (a)

    What happens when \(G=0\) and \(B=0\) (pushed all the way to the left) and \(R\) is allowed to vary?
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    What happens when \(R=0\) and \(G=0\) (pushed all the way to the left) and \(B\) is allowed to vary?
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    What vector represents black in the \(RGB\) color model? What vector represents white?
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    What happens when \(C_b=0\) and \(C_r=0\) (kept in the center) and \(Y\) is allowed to vary?
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    What happens when \(Y=0\) (pushed left) and \(C_r=0\) (kept in the center) and \(C_b\) is allowed to increase from 0 to 127.5?
    🔗
    🔗

    (f)

    What vector represents black in the \(YC_bC_r\) color model? What vector represents white?
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    (a)

    Find the matrix \(P_{\bcal}\) that converts \(\threevec Y{C_b}{C_r}\) into \(\threevec RGB\) (that is \(P_{\bcal}\threevec{Y}{C_b}{C_r}=\threevec RGB\)). You may want to give this a name in Sage; that is, Pb = matrix(...).
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Find the matrix that converts \(\threevec RGB\) into \(YC_bC_r\text{.}\) You may also want to give this a name in Sage.
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Find the \(YC_bC_r\) coordinates for the following colors and use the diagrams to check that the two representations agree.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \threevec RGB = \threevec{255}00, \hspace*{24pt} \threevec RGB = \threevec0{255}0, \hspace*{24pt} \threevec RGB = \threevec{255}{255}{255} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    Find the \(RGB\) coordinates for the following colors and use the diagrams to check that the two representations agree.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \threevec Y{C_b}{C_r} = \threevec {128}00, \hspace*{24pt} \threevec Y{C_b}{C_r} = \threevec {128}{60}0, \hspace*{24pt} \threevec Y{C_b}{C_r} = \threevec {128}0{60}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    Write an expression for the luminance \(Y\) as it depends on \(R\text{,}\) \(G\text{,}\) and \(B\text{.}\) Explain how the luminance represents the brightness of the color.
    🔗
    🔗

    (f)

    Write an expression for the blue chrominance \(C_b\) in terms of \(R\text{,}\) \(G\text{,}\) and \(B\text{.}\) Explain how the blue chrominance measures the amount of blue in the color.
